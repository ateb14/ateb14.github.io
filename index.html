Probability Flow ODE

Posted on 2023-06-08 In Generative Models

Probability Flow ODE

Definition

Consider the following SDE:

\[ d\bold{x} = \bold{f}(\bold{x},t)dt + \bold{G}(\bold{x},t)d\bold{w}, \]

where \(\bold{f}(\cdot,t): \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d, \bold{G}(\cdot,t): \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\).

The marginal probability density \(p_t(\bold{x})\) that is produced by the given ODE is equivalent to the marginal probability density \(p_t(\bold{x})\) that is generated by the SDE mentioned above.

\[ d\bold{x} = \bold{\widetilde{f}}(\bold{x},t)dt,\\ \bold{\widetilde{f}}(\bold{x},t) = \bold{f}(\bold{x},t) - \frac{1}{2}\nabla\cdot[\bold{G}(\bold{x},t)\bold{G}(\bold{x},t)^T] - \frac{1}{2}\bold{G}(\bold{x},t)\bold{G}(\bold{x},t)^T\nabla_{x}\log p_t(\bold{x}) \]

Application in SDE Driven Diffusion

For a simplified forward process SDE used in SMLD:

\[ d\bold{x} = \bold{f}_t(x)dt+g_td\bold{w}. \]

Its probability flow ODEs is:

\[ d\bold{x} = [\bold{f}_t(x)-\frac{1}{2}g_t^2\nabla_x \log p_t(x)]dt. \]

And the reverse SDE(ODE) for it is also \(d\bold{x} = [\bold{f}_t(x)-\frac{1}{2}g_t^2\nabla_x \log p_t(x)]dt\) .

It is possible to obtain \(x_T\) from \(x_0\) using this ODE in a deterministic manner. Similarly, it is also possible to deterministically construct \(x_0\) from \(x_T\) using the reverse process. This is equivalent to the Flow Model, which decodes the latent variables (noise) using a reversible transformation.

Probabilistic flow ODE allows us to relate the results of diffusion models with the results of flow models, such as accurately calculating likelihood and obtaining representations of latent variables. These are essentially the benefits of flow models mentioned in the original paper. Due to the reversibility of flow models, it also allows us to do image editing on the original image in the latent variable space.

Insights

以下是我对Probability Flow ODE的个人见解：从宏观的统计角度来说，对于一个SDE驱动的系统（此处假设为thermodynamics中的分子运动场景），SDE描述了在给定零时刻的粒子位置概率分布的情况下，之后任意的时刻\(t\)中粒子位置分布，这是一个统计意义上信息，具体来说就是我们在\(t\)时刻任意选定一个粒子（对其未有其他已知信息），其可能出现的位置分布就是\(p_t(\bold{x})\)。

对于概率流ODE来说，其描述了另一个确定性的动力系统，其随时间演化的边缘分布\(\hat{p}_t(\bold{x})\)与SDE描述的相同，也就是说，在我们对整个ODE系统未知的时候，我们在\(t\)时刻采样一个粒子的位置，其可能出现位置的概率分布和SDE系统的边缘分布相同，而由于ODE的确定性，我们一旦确认了任意一个时刻某一个粒子的位置，我们就能回溯并预知其过去和未来的所有位置，所以概率流ODE的唯一不确定性来自于未观测，一旦进行了观测，我们就能确定一个粒子所有时刻的位置，也就是\(p_t(\bold{x}|\bold{x}_{observed}) = \delta(\bold{x}-\bold{x}_{t,observed})\)，其中\(\bold{x}_{t,observed}\)由观测值根据ODE唯一确定。

而对SDE系统来说，一次观测并无法消除所有的不确定性，即使我们在\(t\)时刻下对一个粒子的位置进行采样观测到了其位置，其之前之后的位置依旧是不确定的，也即\(p_t(\bold{x}|\bold{x}_{observed})\)无法用狄拉克函数描述，但相比\(p_t(\bold{x})\)其不确定性（例如方差）会小很多。

因此，SDE和其对应的概率流ODE系统的相同之处是从宏观角度上去定义的，即每时每刻的采样（任取一个粒子进行观测）都会服从相同的分布（当采样了同时刻足够多的粒子后，其频率分布应该是基本一致的），这是一个群体上的宏观性质，但一旦我们进行了一次观测后试图追溯同一个粒子的轨迹时，事实上两个系统的表现是不同的。

一个值得注意的事实就是，DDIM采样过程确定的ODE其实就是概率流ODE的一个特例。

Here is my personal view on Probability Flow ODE: From a macroscopic statistical perspective, for a system driven by SDE (assuming a scenario of molecular motion in thermodynamics), SDE describes the probability distribution of particle positions at any given time t, given the probability distribution of particle positions at time zero. Specifically, if we randomly select a particle at time \(t\) (without any other known information about it), its possible position distribution is \(p_t(x)\).

For Probability Flow ODE, it describes another deterministic dynamic system with the same marginal distribution of SDE evolving over time, denoted as \(\hat{p}_t(x)\). That is, when we have no knowledge about the entire ODE system, the probability distribution of a particle's position at time \(t\), given a sample at time zero, is the same as that of the SDE system. Since ODE is deterministic, once we determine the position of a particle at any given time, we can trace back and predict all its past and future positions. Therefore, the only uncertainty of Probability Flow ODE comes from the unobserved particles. Once observed, we can determine the position of a particle at all times, denoted as \(p_t(x| x_{observed}) = \delta(x - x_{t, observed})\), where \(x_{t, observed}\) is uniquely determined by the observed value according to ODE.

For the SDE system, a single observation cannot eliminate all uncertainties. Even if we observe the position of a particle at time t, its positions before and after the observation are still uncertain, i.e., \(p_t(x| x_{observed})\) cannot be described using the Dirac delta function. However, compared to \(p_t(x)\), its uncertainty (such as variance) will be much smaller.

Therefore, the similarity between SDE and its corresponding Probability Flow ODE system is defined from a macroscopic perspective, that is, each sampling at any given time (randomly selecting a particle for observation) will follow the same distribution (when enough particles are sampled simultaneously, their frequency distribution should be basically consistent). This is a macroscopic property of the group. However, once we try to trace back the trajectory of the same particle after one observation, the performance of the two systems is different.

An important fact to note is that the ODE determined by the DDIM sampling process is actually a special case of Probability Flow ODE.

Derivation

The marginal probability density evolves according to Kolmogorov’s forward equation (Fokker-Planck equation)

\[ \frac{\partial p_t(\bold{x})}{\partial t} = -\sum_{i=1}^{d}\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}[f_i(\bold{x},t)p_t(\bold{x})] + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{d}\sum_{j=1}^{d}\frac{\partial ^2}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}[p_t(\bold{x})G^i(\bold{x},t)G^j(\bold{x},t)^T],\\ G(\bold{x},t) = \begin{bmatrix} G^1(\bold{x},t) \\ ...\\ G^d(\bold{x},t) \end{bmatrix} \]

Sliced Score Matching

Last edited time: May 19, 2023 4:54 PM Owner: YY W Tags: Technique

Background

The optimization object of score matching is

\[ \begin{aligned} \int &p(\bold{x})[\frac{1}{2}||\nabla_x\log p_\theta (\bold{x})||^2 + tr(\nabla_x^2\log p_\theta (\bold{x}))]d\bold{x} \\&= \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[\frac{1}{2}||\nabla_x\log p_\theta (\bold{x})||^2 + tr(\nabla_x^2\log p_\theta (\bold{x}))] \\&= \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[\frac{1}{2}||s_\theta(x)||^2 + tr(\nabla_\bold{x}s_\theta (\bold{x}))].\end{aligned} \]

Derivation of score matching can be found on Score Matching.

However, naive score matching is NOT scalable. Suppose that we train a deep neural network which outputs a d-dimension vector as the estimation of the score function. Computing \(tr(\nabla_\bold{x}s_\theta(\bold{x}))\) requires \(O(d)\) backpropagation since we have to calculate

\[ \{ \frac{\partial s_{\theta,i}(x)}{\partial x_i}| i\in\{1,2,...,d\}\}. \]

Sliced Score Matching

Project the score function onto random vectors \(\bold{v}\), which introduces the Sliced Fisher Divergence:

\[ \frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}_{v\sim p(v)}\mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[||\bold{v}^T\nabla_{\bold{x}}\log p_\theta(\bold{x})-\bold{v}^T\nabla_{\bold{x}}\log p(\bold{x})||^2] \\ = \mathbb{E}_{v\sim p(v)}\mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[\bold{v}^T\nabla_{\bold{x}}^2\log p_\theta(\bold{x})\bold{v}+ \frac{1}{2}[\bold{v}^T\nabla_{\bold{x}}\log p_\theta(\bold{x})]^2] + const \]

When selecting the distribution \(p(\bold{z})\), there is flexibility to choose options such as a multivariate standard normal \(N(0, I)\) or a multivariate Rademacher distribution (which is the uniform distribution over \(\{±1\}^D\)).

In practice, when working with a dataset \(X^N = \{\bold{x}_1, \bold{x}_2,...,\bold{x}_n \}\), we draw \(M\) random projections from \(p_\bold{v}\) for each point \(\bold{x}_i\) independently. The collection of all these vectors are denoted by \(V^{NM} = \{ \bold{v}_{ij}\}_{1\le i\le N, 1\le j\le M}\). The estimator can be defined as{

\[ \hat{J}(\theta;X^N,V^{NM}) = \frac{1}{N}\frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{i=1}^{M}[\bold{v}_{i,j}^T\nabla_\bold{x}s(\bold{x};\theta)\bold{v}_{ij} +\frac{1}{2}\bold{v}_{ij}^Ts(\bold{x};\theta))^2] \]

When \(p_{\bold{v}}\) is a multivariate standard normal or multivariate Rademacher distribution, we have

\(\mathbb{E}_{\bold{z}\sim p(\bold{z})}[(\bold{v}_{ij}^Ts(\bold{x};\theta))^2] = ||s(\bold{x};\theta)||^2_2\), which may lead to a simplified estimator with lower variance:

\[ \hat{J}_{vr}(\theta;X^N,V^{NM}) = \frac{1}{N}\frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{i=1}^{M}[\bold{v}_{i,j}^T\nabla_\bold{x}s(\bold{x};\theta)\bold{v}_{ij} +\frac{1}{2}||s(\bold{x};\theta)||_2^2] \]

The author refers to this version as sliced score matching with variance reduction(SSM-VR), and it has better performance empirically.

If we train a neural network \(h_\theta(\bold{x})\) to predict the score function, then we only need \(O(1)\) backpropagation to calculate \(\nabla_{\bold{x}}[\bold{v}^Th_{\theta}(\bold{x})] =\bold{v}^T\nabla_{\bold{x}}h_{\theta}(\bold{x})\) .

Reference

[1] Song et al(2019). Sliced Score Matching: A Scalable Approach to Density and Score Estimation.

Consistency Model

Background

Probability Flow ODE

Consider the following SDE used in diffusion model:

\[ d\bold{x_t} = \bold{\mu}(\bold{x_t},t)dt+\sigma(t)\bold{w}. \]

A probability flow ODE(PF ODE) shares the same marginal probability distribution as it:

\[ d\bold{x_t} = [\bold{\mu}(\bold{x_t},t)-\frac{1}{2}\sigma(t)^2\nabla_x \log p_t(\bold{x_t})]dt. \]

In this paper, the author proposes using \(\mu(\bold{x},t) = \bold{0}\) and \(\sigma(t) = \sqrt{2t}\) in Karras et al. (2022), which results in the empirical PF ODE:

\[ \frac{d\bold{x}_t}{d_t} = -ts_\phi(\bold{x}_t,t). \]

Definition

Given a solution trajectory solution \(\{\bold{x}_t\}_{t\in[\epsilon,T]}\) of the PF ODE, the consistency function is defined as \(\bold{f}:(\bold{x}_t,t)\mapsto\bold{x}_{\epsilon}\) which has a property called self-consistency: its outputs are consistent for arbitrary pairs of \((\bold{x}_t, t)\) that belong to the same PF ODE trajectory, i.e., \(\bold{f}(\bold{x}_t,t) = \bold{f}(\bold{x}_{t'},t')\) for all \(t,t' \in [ε,T]\).

The aforementioned definition implies a boundary condition where any \(\bold{f}(\cdot,\epsilon)\) is an identity function, meaning that \(\bold{f}(\bold{x}_\epsilon,\epsilon) = \bold{x}_\epsilon\). For consistency models based on deep neural networks, the author proposes using a skip link which enforces the model to respect the boundary condition:

\[ \bold{f}_\theta(\bold{x},t) = c_{skip}(t)\bold{x} +c_{out}F_\theta(\bold{x},t), \]

where \(c_{skip}(t)\) and \(c_{out}(t)\) are differentiable functions \(s.t.\) \(c_{skip}(\epsilon)=1\) and \(c_{out}(\epsilon)=0\).

Sampling

The sampling process involves only one forward pass through the consistency model where a sample from \(\hat{\bold{x}}_T\sim \mathcal{N}(\bold{0},T^2 I)\) is fed to the neural network \(\bold{f}_\theta(\hat{\bold{x}}_T,T)\). (Single Step)

“Importantly, one can also evaluate the consistency model multiple times by alternating denoising and noise injection steps for improved sample quality.”

Training

Consistency Distillation(CD)

Loss Function:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{CD}^N(\theta,\theta^-;\phi) := \mathbb{E}_{\bold{x} \sim p_{data}}\mathbb{E}_{n \sim \mathcal{U}[0,N-1]}[\lambda(n)d(\bold{f}_\theta(\bold{x}_{t_{n+1}},t_{n+1}),\bold{f}_{\theta^-}(\bold{\hat{x}}_{t_n}^\phi,t_{n}))], \]

where \(\bold{x}_{t_{n+1}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\bold{0},t_{n+1}^2)\) and \(\bold{\hat{x}}_{t_n}^\phi\) is estimated using a ODE solver given a pre-trained score model \(s_\phi(\bold{x},t)\):

\[ \bold{\hat{x}}_{t_n}^\phi := \bold{x}_{t_{n+1}} - (t_n-t_{n+1})t_{n+1}s_\phi(\bold{x}_{t_{n+1}},t_{n+1}). \]

\(\theta^-\) denotes the running average(EMA) of the past arguments \(\theta\) during optimization:

\[ \theta^- \leftarrow stopgrad(\mu \theta^- + 1-\mu)\theta). \]

Consistency Training(CT)

There exists an unbiased estimator of \(\nabla\log p_t(\bold{x}_t)\):

\[ \nabla\log p_t(\bold{x}_t) = -\mathbb{E}_{\bold{x}_t\sim\mathcal{N}(\bold{0},t^2I)}[\frac{\bold{x}_t-\bold{x}}{t^2}]. \]

Loss Function:

\[ \mathcal L_{CD}^N(\theta,\theta^-;\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{\bold{x} \sim p_{data}}\mathbb{E}_{n \sim \mathcal{U}[0,N-1]}[\lambda(n)d(\bold{f}_\theta(\bold{x}+t_{n+1}\bold{z},t_{n+1}),\bold{f}_{\theta^-}(\bold{x}+t_{n}\bold{z},t_{n}))], \]

where \(z\sim \mathcal N(\bold{0},I)\).

Assume that the pre-trained score model matches the ground truth \(s.t.\) \(s_\phi(\bold{x},t) \equiv \nabla \log p_t(\bold{x})\), with some additional boundary restrictions, then:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{CD}^N(\theta,\theta^-;\phi) = \mathcal{L}_{CT}^N(\theta,\theta^-;\phi) + o(\Delta t), \]

where \(\Delta t = \max_{n\in[1,N-1]}\{ |t_{n+1} - t_n|\}\).

Foundation of Generative Models

Foundation of Generative Models

Definition

Given observational data sampled from the true distribution \(p(x)\), denoted as \(x \sim p(x)\), training (parameter estimation) can be conducted to obtain a generative model \(p_{\theta}(x)\) controlled by \(\theta\) that approximates the true distribution.

Difficulties

Generally speaking, we cannot directly sample \(x\) from \(p_{\theta}(x)\), but it is easy to sample from a uniform distribution or a Gaussian distribution (using re-parameterization trick) to obtain data. How to use a simple distribution or other methods for sampling is also a consideration in the modeling process.

Models

Latent Variable Models

\(p(x) = \int p(x|z)p(z)dz\), where \(z\) follows the distribution \(p(z)\). \(z\) is called a latent variable, and \(p(z)\) is a prior probability distribution that can be arbitrarily specified. A model of this form is called a latent variable model.

For a single latent variable model, we can approximate \(p(x|z)\) by \(p_{\theta}(x|z)\). Once we have done so, it is easy to compute \(p_{\theta}(x) = \int p_{\theta}(x|z)p(z)dz\). By using the prior \(p(z)\), we can approximate the joint probability \(p(x,z)\), which is equivalent to approximating the likelihood \(p(x|z)\) since \(p(x,z) = p(x|z)p(z)\).

\[ \begin{aligned}KL(p(x,z)||p_\theta(x,z)) &= \int\int p(x,z)log\frac{p(x,z)}{p_\theta(x,z)}dzdx\\ &= \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[logp(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[\int p(z|x)log\frac{p(z|x)}{p_\theta(x,z)}dz] \\ &=C + L\mathbb{} \\ \\L &= KL(p(x,z)||p_\theta(x,z)) - C \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[\int p(z|x)log\frac{p(z|x)}{p_\theta(x,z)}dz]\end{aligned} \]

Minimizing \(KL(p(x,z)||p_\theta(x,z))\) is equivalent to minimizing \(L\), which is called the evidence lower bound (ELBO).

(Actually it’s the negative of ELBO: \(\mathbb{E}_{z\sim p(z|x)}[\int p(z|x)log\frac{p_\theta(x,z)}{p(z|x)}dz] \le \log p_\theta(x)\) )

\[ \begin{align} L &= \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[\int p(z|x)log\frac{p(z|x)}{p_\theta(x,z)}dz] \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[\mathbb{E}_{z\sim p(z|x)}[-log p_\theta(x|z)] + KL(p(z|x)||p(z)) ] \end{align} \]

We refer to the neural network approximating \(p_\theta(x|z)\) as the decoder, and the neural network approximating \(p_\theta(z|x)\) as the encoder.

First, let's look at the first term of the optimization function L, where \(p_\theta(x|z)\) is the likelihood function. Therefore, minimizing \(-log p_\theta(x|z)\) is equivalent to maximizing the likelihood function. The second term aims to make the true posterior distribution \(p(z|x)\) as close as possible to the prior distribution \(p(z)\), so that the \(z\) sampled from the prior distribution can correspond to the data \(x\) and be fed into the decoder for better generation.

There are still some difficulties:

  • How to generate \(x\)? According to \(p_{\theta}(x) = \int p_{\theta}(x|z)p(z)dz\), we would need to sample an infinite number of \(z\) values to obtain \(p_\theta(x)\).
  • If we already have \(p_\theta(x)\), it remains unclear how to sample \(x\) from it.

Some possible solutions:

  • We can view \(p_\theta(x|z)\) as generating \(x\) from \(z\).
  • We can use \(\mu = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p_\theta (x|z)}[x]\) to represent \(x\) generated by \(z\).

Then here comes some targets:

  • We can derive the maximum likelihood and expectation from the likelihood function \(p_\theta(x|z)\). This means that the formulation should be "nice".
  • The form of the posterior \(p(z|x)\) is sufficient for us to calculate its KL divergence with the prior \(p(z)\).

Some additional information：

An intuitive explanation for the need of a latent variable and how we can circumvent the prohibitively expensive integral \(p_{\theta}(x) = \int p_{\theta}(x|z)p(z)dz\) can be found on Wikipedia:

\(X\): observable \(X,Z\) \(Z\): latent
\(p^*(x)≈p_θ(x)≈\frac{p_θ(x|z)p(z)}{q_ϕ(z|x)}\) approximable \(p(z)\), easy
\(p_θ(x|z)p(z)\), easy
\(p_θ(z|x)≈q_ϕ(z|x)\) approximable \(p_θ(x|z)\), easy

Autoencoder

The idea behind autoencoders is that the latent variable z and x can uniquely correspond to each other. The mapping from \(x\) to \(z\) belongs to feature dimensionality reduction, while the mapping from \(z\) to \(x\) belongs to feature reconstruction. This is equivalent to the mapping between high-dimensional space and low-dimensional manifold, which belongs to a single latent variable model, and the latent variable space is continuous and uniform.

  • Posterior: \(p(z|x)= \delta(z-C(x))\), where \(C(x)\) is the true encoder and \(\delta(x)\) is the Dirac delta function.
  • Prior: \(p(z) = \frac{1}{D}, 0\le z\le D\).
  • Likelihood: \(p_\theta (x|z) = N(G_\theta(z), \sigma^2I)\), where \(G_\theta(z)\) is the decoder.

Variational Autoencoder(VAE)

VAE supposes that each \(x\) should correspond to a distribution, and this distribution can be used to regenerate \(x\).

  • Prior: \(p(z) = N(0,I)\)
  • Posterior: \(p(z|x) = N(\mu,\sigma^2I), p_\theta(z|x) = N(C_\theta(x),\sigma^2I)\)
  • Likelihood: \(p_\theta (x|z) = N(G_\theta(z), \sigma^2I)\), where \(G_\theta(z)\) is the decoder.

An intuitive explanation from zhihu:

During training, since each \(x\) corresponds to a Gaussian distribution, there are many Gaussian distributions \(p_\theta(z|x)\) that aim to approximate \(p(z)\). As a result, during sampling, a \(z\) value may be “disputed” by multiple Gaussian distributions \(p_\theta(x|z)\), and it may end up resembling the one with the closest mean, creating a "chimera" that generates diversity while still achieving the desired outcome.

Diffusion

In contrast to the aforementioned single latent variable model, diffusion models involve several latent variables \(z_1,...,z_T\). The distribution of \(x\) is expressed using a Markov Chain approach.

\[ p(x) = \int p(x|z_1)p(z_1|z_2)...p(z_{T-1}|z_T)p(z_T)dz_{1:T} \]

Then the “Bayesian components” can be indicated as follows:

  • Prior: \(p(z_T) = N(0,I)\)
  • Posterior: \(p(z_T|x)= N(\mu, \sigma^2I), p(z_{t}|z_{t-1}) = N(\sqrt{\alpha_t}z_{t-1}, \beta_t)\) (In DDPM).
  • Likelihood: \(p(x|z_T) = p(x|z_1)p(z_1|z_2)...p(z_{T-1}|z_T)\)

We observe that the encoder(posterior) here is unlearnable(fixed) and as \(T\rightarrow \infty\), \(p(z_T,x)\rightarrow N(0,I) = p(z_T)\).

Flow-based Models

Flow models aim to establish a one-to-one correspondence between \(x\) and the latent variable \(z\), similar to the single latent variable model of AE. However, what sets flow models apart is that they also aim for this correspondence to be a bijection.

  • Posterior: \(p(z|x) = \delta(z-C(x))\)
  • Likelihood: \(p(x|z) = \delta(x-G(z)), G = C^{-1}\)
  • Prior: \(p(z) = N(0,I)\)

Summary for Latent Variable Models

  • The concept behind AE is that \(x\) and \(z\) have a one-to-one correspondence, while VAE assumes that \(x\) corresponds to a particular distribution. Both types of models use neural networks to directly fit their encoders and decoders.
  • Like VAE, diffusion also aims for \(x\) to correspond to a certain distribution, but it utilizes a chain-like structure for the encoder instead of directly simulating it with a neural network.
  • The Flow model is comparable to AE in its aim for a unique correspondence between \(x\) and \(z\), but it places greater emphasis on an inverse relationship between the encoder and decoder. As a result, its design is more rigorous than the encoder structure of Diffusion.

Energy-based Models

Consider directly modeling \(p(x)\) as:

\[ p_\theta(x) = \frac{q_\theta(x)}{Z(\theta)} \]

Here, \(Z(\theta) = \int q_\theta(x)dx\), which is known as the normalization constant.

Specifically, if \(q_\theta(x) = e^{-U_\theta(x)}\), then:

\[ p_\theta(x) = \frac{e^{-U_\theta(x)}}{Z(\theta)} \]

Here, \(U_\theta(x)\) is an unspecified function, also known as the energy function; and \(p_\theta(x)\) is called the energy distribution, also known as the Maxwell–Boltzmann Distribution.

Score Matching

Score function \(s_\theta(x)=\nabla_{x}\log p_\theta(x) = \nabla_{x}\log q_\theta(x)\)\(\nabla_{x}\log p_\theta(x) = \nabla_{x}\log q_\theta(x)\), which is irrelevant to \(Z(\theta)\). Estimating the gradient of \(\nabla_{x}\log p_\theta(x)\) can also provide insight into the data distribution, as they only differ by a scaling factor \(Z(\theta)\). This can be helpful in understanding the shape and trend of the data distribution \(p(x)\).

The optimization object can be defined using Fisher divergence:

\[ \begin{aligned} J(\theta) &=\frac{1}{2}\int p(x)||\nabla_{x}\log p_\theta(x) - \nabla_{x}\log p(x) ||^2 dx\\ &= \int p(x)[\frac{1}{2}||s_\theta(x)||^2 -s_\theta(x)^Ts_\theta(x) + \frac{1}{2}||s(x) ||^2]dx\end{aligned} \]

The third term in this equation can be neglected since it’s irrelevant to \(\theta\).p

For the \(i^{th}\) element of the second term here:

\[ \begin{aligned} -\int p(x)s_{\theta,i}(x)s_i(x)dx &= -\int s_{\theta,i}(x)\frac{\partial p(x)}{\partial(x_i)}dx \\ &= -\int[\int s_{\theta, i}(x)\frac{\partial p(x)}{\partial(x_i)}dx_i]dx_{1:n-\{i\}} \\ &= -\int[s_{\theta,i}(x)p(x)\bigg|_{x = -\infty}^{x=+\infty}-\int p(x)\frac{\partial s_{\theta,i}(x)}{\partial(x_i)} dx_i]dx_{1:n-\{i\}} \\ &= \int p(x)\frac{\partial s_{\theta,i}(x)}{\partial(x_i)}dx_{1:n} \end{aligned} \]

Then the second term is:

\[ \begin{aligned} -\int p(x)s_\theta(x)^Ts_\theta(x) dx&=\int p(x)tr(\nabla_\bold{x}s_\theta(x))dx_{1:n} \end{aligned} \]

Then the optimization object is :

\[ \begin{aligned} \int &p(\bold{x})[\frac{1}{2}||\nabla_x\log p_\theta (\bold{x})||^2 + tr(\nabla_x^2\log p_\theta (\bold{x}))]d\bold{x} \\&= \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[\frac{1}{2}||\nabla_x\log p_\theta (\bold{x})||^2 + tr(\nabla_x^2\log p_\theta (\bold{x}))] \\&= \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p(x)}[\frac{1}{2}||s_\theta(x)||^2 + tr(\nabla_\bold{x}s_\theta (\bold{x}))].\end{aligned} \]

Here \(\nabla_x^2\log p_\theta (\bold{x})\) denotes the Hessian Matrix of \(\log p_\theta (\bold{x})\).

LU factorization

We can factorize an invertible square matrix \(A\) into two triangular matrices \(L,U\), where \(U\) is an upper triangular matrix with pivots on its diagonal, and \(L\) is a lower triangular matrix with \(L_{i,j}\) exactly equal to the multiplier that multiplied the pivot row \(j\) when it was subtracted from row \(i\) in elimination to solve \(Ax = b\). \[ A = LU \] $L $ stands for the forward elimination step turning \(A\) into an upper triangular matrix: \[ LUx = b \Rightarrow Ux = L^{-1}b=c \Rightarrow Lc = b \] \(U\) stands for the backward substitution that gives the final answer after we solve \(c\) From \(Lc = b\): \[ Ux = c \] For example, when \(A = \left[ \begin{matrix} 1&2\\4&9 \end{matrix} \right], b = [5,21]^{T}\), \(L = \left[\begin{matrix} 1&0\\4&1 \end{matrix}\right], U = \left[\begin{matrix} 1&2\\0&1 \end{matrix}\right]\), for we turn \(Ax=b\) into \(x+2y=5,y=1\),which can also be written in the format: \(Ux = c\) that yields \(U\), using the forward elimination that yields \(L\). In all, the whole process to solve \(Ax=b\) using LU factorization can be denoted as: \[ L \Rightarrow (c,U) \Rightarrow x \]

The diagonal of \(L\) comprises all 1's, but \(U\) probably doesn't. We can further factorize \(A\) into \(LDU\) by scaling all the pivots of \(U\) into one, where D is a diagonal matrix containing the pivots of the previous \(U\)（\(A\)).

It's noteworthy that when \(A^{T} = A\), \(A = LDU = LDL^T\), which will save almost half of the time and space to solve \(Ax=b\).

Projections

We have \(n\) vectors \(a_1,...,a_n\) in \(R^m\) that are linearly independent and a vector \(b \in R^m\). Find the vector \(p=A\hat{x}\) in the column space of \(A = <a_n,...,a_n>\) which is closest to \(b\) and the projection matrix \(P\) which statisfies \(p = Pb\).

  • Subspace: \(C(A)\)
  • The error vector \(b - A\hat{x}\) is perpendicular to this column space
  • Consequently \(b-A\hat{x}\) is in the nullspace of \(A^T\), which means that \(A^T(b-A\hat{x}) = 0\)

Therefore, \(p = A\hat{x} = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^Tb \Rightarrow P = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T\).

We have \(P^2 = P\) for the second projection won't change the first projection.

It's noteworthy that \(A^TA\) is invertible if and only if \(A\) has linearly independent columns and we can prove it by proving that the nullspace of \(A\) is exactly that of \(A^TA\).

QR factorization

\(A = QR\), where the columns of \(A\) are linearly independent and \(Q\) is an orthonormal matrix that comes from the Gram-Schmidt method(calculating the orthogonal bases by subtracting the projections on previous bases), i.e. \(Q^TQ = I\). Therefore, \(R = Q^TA\), and \(q_{i}\) is orthogonal to \(a_1,...,a_{i-1}\), which means that \(R\) is an upper triangular matrix.

Diagonalization in Differential Equation

We try to solve this constant-coefficient differential equation starting from \(\bold{u}(0)\): \[ \frac{d\bold{u}}{dt} = A\bold{u} \] If \(Ax = \lambda x\), we get \(\frac{d\bold{u}}{dt} = \lambda \bold{u}\) when \(\bold{u} = e^{\lambda t}\bold{x}\).

When \(A\) is diagonalizable, the complete solution is \(\bold{u(t)} = \sum_{i=1}^{i=n}c_ie^{\lambda_{i t}}\bold{x_i}\).

Even when there is a shortage of eigenvectors, we can let \(\bold{u} = e^{At}\bold{u}(0)\).

  • \(e^{At} = I + At + \frac{1}{2}(At)^2...\)
  • \(\frac{de^{At}}{dt} = Ae^{At}\)
  • Eigenvalues are \(e^{\lambda t}\) for \(e^{At}x = (1+\lambda t+...)x\)

LegoOS

Posted on 2022-12-10 In operating system

Writeup for LegoOS: A Disseminated, Distributed OS for Hardware Resource Disaggregation

What is this paper talking about?

This is a definition paper which introduces a new OS design paradigm, LegoOS, which decouples the process, memory and storage components in a system, where any of which communicates with others only through network and the failure of a single hardware won't affect others.

What is the motivation?

Previous servers, especially monolithic servers, suffer from its lack of flexibility as different types of hardwares are clustered together through local bus, resulting in several key limitations:

  • Inefficient resource utilization resulted from the constraint that CPU and memory for a job has to be allocated from the same physical machine.
  • Poor hardware elasticity to configure the hardware components.
  • Coarse failure domain which will fail the whole sever when only a single hardware component fails.
  • Bad support for heterogeneity which makes it hard to add more new hardware devices.

With the rapid growth of network speed, it's possible to separate different hardware components and connect them with network instead of local bus. These hardware components thus compose the whole system like Lego.

General ideas

The whole system is composed of three kinds of hardware components: process(pComponent), memory(mComponent) and storage(sComponent), each of which owns a monitor that manage the component and communicates with others through network. As a result the functionalities of the system are split.

A monitor runs on its specific hardware component, thus it can use its own implementation to manage the hardware component that it runs on. So to deploy a new hardware device, its developers only need to implement the monitor of the device and simply attach it to the network of the system.

The components of the system are not coherent, and only the coherence that the hardware provides within a component is guaranteed. This is because maintaining coherence between different components will cause high network bandwidth consumption. However, the applications running on the kernel are still able to implement their desired level of coherence.

The kernel also globally manages resources, but only occasionally for coarse-grained decisions to mininize performance and scalability bottleneck, while the monitors of the components will make their own fine-grained decisions.

To handle component failures, the kernel adds redundancy for recovery.

Benefits

  • The hardware devices are loosely coupled, which makes it much more simple to add new hardware devices and configure the hardware components.
  • The recource utilization can be improved by managing the load balance of different hardware components.
  • A failure of a single component won't affect the whole system, which makes the system more reliable.
  • The management policy of a hardware component can be customized, leading to better flexibility of the hardware components.

Drawbacks

  • The resources of a single application are possible to be scattered into different components, which will not only create a locality loss, but also increase the possiblity for a application to suffer from hardware failures.
  • The newwork speed is a bottleneck of LegoOS.

Conclusions

LegoOS demonstrates a new OS paradigm that shows us the possibility to build a highly-scalable and loosely-coupled system based on network. With the development of the network technology, this paradigm is promising.
